Stephen King's 'Salem's Lot' Needs Its Own TV Series

Posted by Aldo Pusey on Monday, January 1, 0001

The Big Picture

  • In Stephen King's novel Salem's Lot , the traditional tough guys in the small town aren't the true heroes and threats, but rather the men who aren't imposing and don't need to prove themselves.
  • The length of Salem's Lot makes it well-suited for a TV series adaptation, as it offers enough material without leaving out important details.
  • The novel not only focuses on different types of men but it also explores the importance of male bonds and the power of found family.

Many of Stephen King’s works have been adapted for the small screen: Under The Dome, The Mist, The Stand, and ‘Salem’s Lot is one of his many works that is ripe for a series. While Salem’s Lot was first adapted into a two-part miniseries in 1979, at 451 pages, the novel is one of King’s lengthier works that is well-suited for a series. The film has been well-received but, like most movies adapted from books, details have been left out or arranged that could leave one feeling that they’re not getting the full experience of the Lot. Its length is long enough to provide enough material for a series but short enough that details wouldn’t have to be left out. Recently, Warner Bros. nearly shelved a theatrical remake of Salem's Lot, but it has been given a streaming release instead.

'Salem's Lot' Is Ripe for a TV Series Adaptation

Published a year after his debut novel, Carrie, King laid the groundwork for a format he would utilize for many of his future works: making full use of a small town. Salem’s Lot takes place in a small fictional town in Maine named Jerusalem’s Lot, which the locals either refer to by the book’s title or simply “the Lot.” The main protagonist is Ben Mears, a somewhat successful novelist who moves back to the Lot where he spent four years as a child to write about the mysterious mansion overlooking the Lot called the Marsten House — a place that holds a traumatic memory for him. The Lot’s residents, suspicious of outsiders, as many small towns are, are curious as to why a writer has made a temporary home of their little Maine town. Ben befriends some of the locals, including Susan Norton with whom he develops a romantic connection.

Ben isn’t the only newcomer to the Lot; foreigners Richard Straker and Kurt Barlow are new arrivals too. They are there to open an antique furniture shop. The Marsten House was purchased by Barlow. Along with being newcomers and seeming to come out of nowhere, Straker and Barlow also raise local eyebrows, with many assuming that they are a couple. Considering it's a small town in the 70s, it's long before things like seeing pride flags hanging from store windows would be common.

But as people have moved in, others have seemed to disappear. First, it’s a little boy. Then his brother falls ill and dies, then another, then another, and then another. Salem’s Lot begins to decrease. At first, it goes unnoticed but then becomes impossible to ignore. Ben and some local men he befriends, including the child Mark Petrie, must figure out what is happening and how to stop it.

'Salem's Lot' Has Vampires, Stephen King's Way

Close

For his second novel, King covered the widely popular genre of vampires. While not the most distinct premise among his novels, he manages to make the genre his own with his unique interpretation of vampires. In a prologue for Salem’s Lot, King says he didn’t want to make his vampires like the wine snobs that so many vampires appear as. His vampires aren’t Dracula, they’re Nosferatu. King, unsurprisingly, doesn’t shy away from gore, and his bloodsuckers are monsters and nothing more. Anne Rice’s vampires were humanized; Stephanie Meyer’s vampires were sexualized; but King’s serve only to terrorize.

Vampires have become common in films and television: Buffy the Vampire Slayer’s vampires are prosthetic-clad embodiments of adolescent trials. The Vampire Diaries followed the vampire romance craze ignited by Twilight. In What We Do in the Shadows' fanged protagonists are followed around mockumentary-style from one folly to the next. King’s vampires could break from the tradition of making vampires sexually appealing. Both versions of Fright Night have attractive men (Chris Sarandon in the original and Colin Farrell in the remake) inviting women to their house under the guise of a good — and unfortunately for them, last — night. Interview with the Vampire depicts its killers as masters of eroticism. A series focused on vampires taking over a small town, without any human-vampire mingling, could be a good back-to-basics approach.

'Salem's Lot' Highlights Unconventional Male Hero Characters

Although there is only really one well-defined female character, Salem's Lot isn't just a book full of men, it’s a book about different types of men who are unconventional. King was writing from a place of experience in Salem’s Lot. Stephen King shares a lot in common with Ben Mears. They are both tall and dark-haired, and they are both writers who grew up in Maine and were raised by women. But Ben isn’t the only complex character. Matt Burke is an English teacher who proves to be a voice of reason when chaos creeps in on the town. Jimmy Cody is a doctor whose courage and intelligence provide a form of defense and counter to the rising evil. Father Callahan is self-sacrificing. And Mark Petrie is intelligent, brave, and knows how to kick ass. They’re men who would typically be overlooked in a crisis, they are hardly the conventional heroes of the story.

It’s the town’s typical tough guys who either don’t last or refuse to take a stand. Parkins Gillespie, the town’s constable, abandons the Lot and its people when he becomes privy to the evil that has taken over. Many of the town’s bullish men such as Nolly Gardener, Reggie Sawyer, and Royce McDougall all fall prey to the vampires. It’s the men who are thinkers, not brash actors, that prove any real threat to the vampires. But much like how schoolyard bully Richie was overthrown and proved not to be the biggest threat by Mark, who is bookish and non-aggressive, it’s the men who aren’t seemingly threatening that are the true sources of mayhem. Strickland and Barlow, whom the town rumors to be lovers and neither of whom exude red-blooded machismo, are the true terrors.

'Salem's Lot' and the Importance of Male Connection

But it’s not just different types of men that Salem’s Lot fixates on, it’s a book about male bonds. Strickland and Barlow are bonded by evil, but it's a bond nonetheless. Ben creates meaningful bonds with Matt, Jimmy, and Father Callahan, all of whom welcome him as an outsider. But perhaps the most profound bond is the one shared between the “tall man” and the boy at the beginning of the novel. Despite not being related by blood, the man and the boy have developed a father-son relationship that exemplifies the need and necessity of male connection.

This bond matters more than the ghouls that stalk the night. Stephen King begins the novel by emphasizing the found family they have in each other with the first sentence: “Almost everyone thought the man and the boy were father and son.” These two characters are able to be vulnerable with each other. The love that has formed between them is shown to be the most powerful element of the novel. Salem’s Lot proves that some bonds are stronger than blood. This would add depth to a television series that shifts Salem's Lot beyond just a horror story.

Salem's Lot will stream on Max in 2024. Until then, you can watch the 1979 miniseries version on Amazon Prime in the U.S.
