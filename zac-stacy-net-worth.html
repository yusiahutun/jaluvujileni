Introduction

You may know Zac Stacy as a former American football running back and domestic abuser, but have you delved deeper into this intriguing character’s life?

For instance, I bet you didn’t know that his favorite sportsman is Neymar, his favorite actor is Robert Downey Jr., and he has a love for driving.

But let’s move on from the fun facts and get stuck in: Stacy’s life is marked with huge wins and terrible losses, so read on to learn more about them!

The Origin Story

It all started on April 9th, 1991, when Zachary Latrell Stacy was welcomed into the world in Centreville, Alabama.

As he grew up, Stacy quickly took an interest in American football.

Eventually, he was offered a scholarship by Vanderbilt after developing his sporting talent at Bibb County High School.

In fact, during his high school period, he made 26 touchdowns just as a junior and took a stab at playing quarterback, wide receiver, and running back.

As a senior, Stacy scored a further 36 touchdowns and went on to receive honors, such as being named to the Birmingham News All-State Second Team.

He also twice became Division 4A Player of the Year and Alabama Player of the Year.

At this point, Stacy was undoubtedly one to watch on the pitch – his physical talent was absolute, and the future of his sporting career looked incredibly bright.

An Emerging Career

@Getty

As Stacy started to make his mark on football further at Vanderbilt, he secured a freshman school record of rushing over 135 yards against Western Carolina in 2009.

The following year, he became an efficient offensive force for the Commodores, whilst 2011 saw him earning Vanderbilt’s Most Valuable Offensive Player award and securing the most rushing attempts since Jermaine Johnson back in 1995.

Furthermore, Stacy was pronounced the SEC Offensive Player of the Week for his staggering Senior Day performance against the Wildcats.

By now, Stacy was establishing his success on the field more and more every day. Indeed, in 2012, the sportsman emerged as the first Vanderbilt player to rush for 1000 yards in back-to-back seasons and also secured his Special Education graduate degree, amongst everything else.

Delving Deeper Into The Profession

@Getty

Stacy kicked off his professional career to the maximum in 2013 when he was drafted in the NFL Draft with the St Louis Rams.

He stuck with the Rams for two years, building up his expertise on the field and testing his luck against other professional players before being traded to the New York Jets.

During his time with the Jets, Stacy suffered from several physical injuries, leading him to retire early from the NFL.

Despite this, Stacy couldn’t keep away from the pitch for long.

In 2018, he became an integral part of the Saskatchewan Roughriders and emerged as their leading rusher in their preseason games.

Regardless of his success, former teammate Tre Mason took Stacy’s spot, leading him to sign with the Memphis Express.

As one of the team’s dark horses, Stacy rushed a total of 12 times for 58 yards in the team’s week one loss to the Birmingham Iron.

In a total of 8 games played, Stacy managed to rush a grand total of 317 yards.

A Stand-Out Scandal

@Getty

However, this American football running back’s life wasn’t wholly golden with success.

In 2021, Stacy was submerged in a controversial scandal when he assaulted his ex-girlfriend whilst their 5-month-old son was in the same room.

The disturbing incident of Stacy pushing the child’s mother across the room was even caught on camera, depicting how she was forced against a TV, which then fell onto her.

Whilst this incident occurred in Florida in Stacy’s ex’s home, he was only actually arrested in Orlando.

He had already left Florida by the time the Orange County Sheriff’s Office had acted.

During the arrest, Stacy claimed that his ex-girlfriend had staged the attack out of bitterness.

Despite this, charges of aggravated battery soon caught up with him, and the football player’s reputation shattered in an instant.

In 2023, Stacy was sentenced to six months in jail and a year of probation.
